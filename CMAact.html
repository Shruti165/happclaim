Consumer Protection Act, 2019 - Details

Introduction

The Consumer Protection Act, 2019 is an Act of the Parliament of India to protect the interests of consumers in India. It was passed by both houses of Parliament in August 2019 and received the assent of the President of India on 9 August 2019. It came into force on 20 July 2020. This act replaced the older Consumer Protection Act, 1986.

Key Objectives

  • To provide for the establishment of consumer councils and other authorities for the settlement of consumers' disputes and for matters connected therewith.
  • To protect the rights of consumers by establishing mechanisms for redressal of their grievances.
  • To ensure that consumers are not exploited by unfair trade practices.

Key Features

  • Establishment of Consumer Protection Authorities: The Act establishes Consumer Disputes Redressal Commissions at the National, State, and District levels.
  • Broadened Definition of "Consumer": The definition of "consumer" has been expanded to include those who purchase goods or services through electronic means.
  • Product Liability: The Act introduces the concept of product liability, which holds manufacturers, service providers, and sellers responsible for any harm caused by defective products or deficient services.
  • Unfair Trade Practices: The Act prohibits unfair trade practices such as false advertising, misleading representations, and hoarding.
  • E-commerce Transactions: The Act includes provisions to regulate e-commerce transactions, recognizing the growing importance of online shopping.
  • Mediation: The Act provides for mediation as an alternative dispute resolution mechanism to facilitate quicker settlement of consumer disputes.

Consumer Rights

The Consumer Protection Act, 2019 recognizes the following rights of consumers:

  • The right to be protected against the marketing of goods and services which are hazardous to life and property.
  • The right to be informed about the quality, quantity, potency, purity, standard and price of goods or services, as the case may be so as to protect the consumer against unfair trade practices.
  • The right to be assured, wherever possible, access to a variety of goods and services at competitive prices.
  • The right to be heard and to be assured that consumer's interests will receive due consideration at appropriate forums.
  • The right to seek redressal against unfair trade practices or restrictive trade practices or unfair exploitation of consumers.
  • The right to consumer education.

Three-Tier Redressal Mechanism

The Act provides a three-tier quasi-judicial mechanism for the redressal of consumer disputes:

  1. District Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission (DCDRC): Handles complaints where the value of the goods or services does not exceed ₹1 crore.
  2. State Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission (SCDRC): Handles complaints where the value of the goods or services exceeds ₹1 crore but does not exceed ₹10 crore.
  3. National Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission (NCDRC): Handles complaints where the value of the goods or services exceeds ₹10 crore.

Central Consumer Protection Authority (CCPA)

The Act establishes the Central Consumer Protection Authority (CCPA) to promote, protect, and enforce the rights of consumers. The CCPA can investigate consumer complaints, initiate class action suits, and issue orders against unfair trade practices.

Conclusion

The Consumer Protection Act, 2019 is a comprehensive legislation aimed at protecting the interests of consumers in India. It modernizes the legal framework for consumer protection, expands the scope of consumer rights, and provides for more effective mechanisms for redressal of consumer grievances.
